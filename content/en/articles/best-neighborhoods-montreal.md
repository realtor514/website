People ask me this question constantly: "Georges, where should I buy in Montreal?" My answer always starts with a question back: "What are you optimizing for?" Because the best neighborhood for a first-time buyer building equity is different from the best one for an investor seeking rental yield, which is different again from the best one for a family prioritizing schools and green space.

That said, after analyzing transaction data, price-per-square-foot trends, rental vacancy rates, and infrastructure investment patterns across Greater Montreal, here are my honest assessments.

## Where I'd Buy for Long-Term Appreciation

**Rosemont-La Petite-Patrie**

Rosemont has already had its big run-up, but it keeps attracting young professionals and families because the fundamentals are strong: walkability, bike infrastructure, excellent transit access, and a genuine neighborhood culture that doesn't feel manufactured. Prices are higher than they were five years ago, but the underlying demand shows no sign of weakening. For a primary residence with a 10+ year horizon, this remains one of my top picks.

**Villeray**

Villeray sits just north of Rosemont and has been quietly catching up in desirability while maintaining slightly lower price points. The commercial arteries along Jarry and Villeray have been transforming over the past few years - better restaurants, independent shops, young families moving in. If Rosemont is at peak pricing, Villeray is still in the middle of its appreciation curve.

**Laval - Duvernay and Sainte-Rose**

I spend a lot of time in Laval's market, and these two sectors consistently outperform my expectations. Duvernay offers large lots, good school options, and relatively easy access to Autoroute 440, while Sainte-Rose has genuine village character with waterfront access that you simply can't replicate. Both are underpriced relative to comparable Montreal Island neighbourhoods.

**Saint-Laurent (Ville Saint-Laurent)**

This is a pick that surprises people. Saint-Laurent has a reputation as a suburban industrial area, but the residential sections - particularly near the new REM stations - are positioned to capture significant price appreciation as transit access improves. I'm watching the blocks within walking distance of future REM stops closely.

**Verdun**

Verdun has undergone a dramatic transformation over the past decade. Waterfront revitalization, improved transit, and a younger demographic are reshaping what was once a working-class neighborhood. Rental demand is strong, prices are still reasonable relative to adjacent NDG, and the trajectory is positive.

## My Honest Caveat
