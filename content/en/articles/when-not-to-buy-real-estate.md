This article will cost me money to write. That's fine.

My job, as I see it, is not to sell you a property. It's to give you the best real estate counsel I'm capable of giving. Sometimes that counsel leads to a purchase that earns me a commission. Sometimes it leads to a conversation where I tell someone to wait - or to not buy at all.

Here are the situations where I genuinely recommend against buying.

## When Your Job Situation Is Unstable

Mortgage lenders require 2 years of consistent income history for salaried employees and typically 2 years of business income for self-employed buyers. But lender requirements are the floor, not the ceiling.

If you're in the first year of a new job, recently became self-employed, or work in a field experiencing significant restructuring, the question isn't just "can you qualify?" It's "will this income still exist in 3 years?" A $500,000 mortgage doesn't care that your position was eliminated.

My advice: wait until you have 18-24 months of stability in your new situation before buying. The property market will still have options. The financial damage from a forced sale during a job crisis is far worse than missing a few months of potential appreciation.

## When Your Emergency Fund Would Disappear

I've met buyers who had exactly enough for a down payment and closing costs - and nothing left. That scenario worries me significantly.

Homeownership generates unexpected expenses. The water heater that fails three weeks after possession. The furnace that needs replacing the first winter. The foundation crack that the inspector noted but underestimated. These things happen, and when they happen to someone with no financial buffer, they create a crisis.

My standard recommendation: you should have your down payment, your closing costs (including welcome tax), AND at least 3-6 months of mortgage payments sitting in a reserve before you buy. If achieving that requires waiting another 6-12 months, that's almost always the right call.

## When You're Buying to Solve an Emotional Problem

I see this more than you'd expect. Someone going through a divorce, a difficult breakup, or a period of significant life upheaval decides to buy a property as a way to establish control, create stability, or prove something to someone.

The intention is understandable. The timing is often terrible.

Major life transitions create distorted decision-making. You may be overweighting permanence and underweighting flexibility. A 25-year mortgage is not a therapy tool. Give yourself time to let the emotional terrain settle before committing to the largest financial decision of your adult life.

## When the Numbers Don't Work at Any Price

Sometimes I analyze a property for a client and the conclusion is clear: at this asking price, and at any price the seller seems willing to accept, the purchase doesn't make financial sense. Either the location doesn't support the price, the building has issues that create future liabilities, or the fundamentals of the deal are unfavorable.

In these cases, my job is to say so. Clearly. Not to find a way to make the deal feel good through selective framing. I'd rather lose a commission and have a client who trusts my analysis than close a transaction that creates regret.

## When You Plan to Move in Less Than 3 Years

This one surprises people. Real estate is a long-term investment, and transaction costs are high. Between welcome tax, notary fees, moving costs, and potential commission on resale, a property purchase typically costs 3-5% of its value to enter and exit. If the market appreciates at 3% per year, you need nearly two years just to break even on transaction costs.

If there's a realistic chance you'll relocate for work, family, or lifestyle reasons within 3 years, the math often favors renting. That's not a failure - it's an accurate reading of your actual situation.

## The Conversation I Have

When I meet with clients for the first time, I ask a question that some find unexpected: "Is there any scenario in which buying right now is not the right move for you?"

Most good brokers, in my view, should be asking some version of this question. If the answer is yes, let's talk about it. If the answer is no, then we have a clear foundation to build a search on.

The commission follows from honest counsel. It doesn't precede it.
