There is a sequence to how buyers experience a first property visit. They walk in and their gut registers an immediate response: yes or no. If it is yes, they start to imagine their life there, which makes it harder to see the problems. If it is no, they often leave quickly without gathering useful information about why the property did not work.

Neither response is particularly useful on its own. Here is how to make a first visit genuinely informative regardless of your initial gut reaction.

## Before You Walk In

Arrive early and spend five minutes outside the property. Look at:

**The street.** Is it well-maintained? Are neighboring properties cared for? What is the traffic situation? What is nearby that did not show up in the listing photographs?

**The exterior.** Gutters, downspouts, roof visible from street level, foundation where visible from outside, condition of the driveway and walkway. Deferred exterior maintenance is a signal.

**The parking.** If there is a driveway or garage, is it adequate for your needs? Is street parking realistic?

## Inside the Property: Room by Room

**Entry and main floor:**
- Do doors open and close properly? Sticking doors indicate settlement or moisture swelling.
- Check the floors: are they level? Soft spots under carpet indicate subfloor issues.
- Look at the baseboards and where the walls meet the floor: gaps or separation indicate movement.

**Kitchen:**
- Turn on faucets and check water pressure and drainage speed.
- Check under the sink for evidence of previous leaks.
- Test the exhaust fan.
- Open the dishwasher and check for mold or water damage at the base.

**Bathrooms:**
- Turn on the shower and check water pressure and hot water arrival time.
- Flush the toilet and observe how quickly it fills and whether it runs.
- Look at the caulking around the tub and shower: deteriorated caulk allows water infiltration into the wall cavity.
- Check the ceiling of any bathroom for signs of water staining from above.

**Bedrooms:**
- Open closet doors and smell. Musty odors indicate moisture.
- Check window condition: operation, sealing, and any fogging of double-pane glass (indicates seal failure).

**Basement:**
- This is the most important room. Go slowly. Look at every wall at floor level for efflorescence (white mineral deposits), staining, or cracking.
- Smell the air carefully.
- Find the electrical panel and note the brand and amperage.
- Locate the water heater and furnace and note their visible age.
- Check the floor for cracks, sloping, or dampness.
- Look at the joists visible above you: any staining, rot, or notching that reduces structural strength?

## Questions to Ask the Listing Broker

Ask every question in writing, either by email or text, so there is a record:

"Has there ever been water infiltration in the basement or any area of the property?"
"Have there been any insurance claims in the last five years?"
"How old is the roof? When was it last replaced?"
"Are there any permits for renovations done on the property, and are they closed?"
"What are the monthly utility costs?"

## After You Leave

Write your notes immediately. Photograph anything notable. Compare the list price to the comparables you should have reviewed before the visit.

If your gut says yes and your notes do not identify disqualifying issues, the next step is a formal inspection. The inspection does not replace your first-visit observations. It confirms and expands them.

If your gut says yes and your notes identify significant concerns, discuss those concerns with your broker before allowing emotion to override them. The concerns are information. They either change the price conversation, the condition conversation, or the go/no-go decision. All three outcomes are useful.
