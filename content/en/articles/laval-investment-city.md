I bought my own triplex in Laval. That fact tells you something about where I put my money when I'm making personal real estate decisions.

Here's the case I make for Laval to investors who are fixated on the Montreal island - and it's an argument built on numbers, not sentiment.

## The Price-Per-Square-Foot Advantage

Laval consistently trades at a discount to comparable neighborhoods on the Montreal island. A well-maintained triplex in Duvernay or Vimont will typically cost you 10-20% less per square foot than an equivalent property in Rosemont or Plateau.

That discount exists for partly historical reasons - Laval spent decades being perceived as "suburban" and less desirable than island neighborhoods. That perception has been eroding steadily, which is precisely the dynamic that creates investment opportunity.

## The Rental Demand Story

Laval's population has been growing faster than Montreal's. The city attracts families looking for more space at lower cost, new immigrants establishing themselves in the greater region, and professionals who work in Laval's expanding commercial and industrial sectors.

This demographic growth creates sustained rental demand. Vacancy rates in Laval's established residential sectors have remained structurally low. For income property investors, consistent demand is worth more than a prestigious address.

## Infrastructure Investment

The REM (Réseau express métropolitain) dramatically changes Laval's connectivity story. With REM stations in the western parts of the island connecting to Deux-Montagnes and the broader network, commute times to downtown Montreal from strategic Laval locations are increasingly competitive.

Autoroute 440, 13, 15, 19, and 25 all serve Laval, giving it transportation access that genuinely competes with urban neighborhoods. As transit improves, the historical discount to island prices has less justification.

## The School Factor (Underrated)

Laval has strong French-language public schools, several well-regarded private institutions, and Université Laval's campus presence. For families - who make up a significant portion of rental and purchase demand - educational infrastructure matters enormously.

When I do analysis for clients considering income properties in Laval's family-oriented sectors, the school quality in the catchment area is one of the variables I weight explicitly. It drives both rental demand and resale value.

## My Favorite Sectors Right Now

**Duvernay:** Large lots, established trees, good school options, close to commercial corridors. The property stock here is aging but often structurally solid. The renovation potential is real.

**Sainte-Rose:** Village character, waterfront access on the Rivière des Mille Îles, genuine charm that you can't replicate in newer developments. Properties here have strong appeal to a specific buyer profile that pays a premium.

**Laval-des-Rapides:** Proximity to the Autoroute 15 exchange and solid transit connections. Slightly denser urban character than other Laval sectors. Active appreciation.

**Auteuil:** Family-oriented, newer housing stock, strong demand from buyers priced out of Duvernay and Sainte-Rose.

## The Honest Counterargument

Laval is not without trade-offs. Parts of the city lack the walkability and neighborhood density that urban buyers value. Some sectors are heavily car-dependent. And the island still commands a premium for buyers who specifically want urban living.

But for investors focused on cash flow, appreciation potential, and long-term demand fundamentals - the data has been pointing to Laval for years. The conversation just hasn't caught up yet.
