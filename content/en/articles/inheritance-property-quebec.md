Inheriting property is almost always complicated. It occurs in the context of loss, often involves multiple heirs with different financial situations and preferences, and requires navigating legal processes that most people have never encountered before.

Here is a practical guide to what happens and what your options are.

## The Quebec Succession Process

When a person dies in Quebec, their estate is handled through succession. If there is a will, the terms of the will govern who receives what. If there is no will, Quebec's Civil Code establishes a default succession order (spouse, children, then more distant relatives in sequence).

The estate is administered by a liquidator (liquidateur), the person designated in the will or appointed by the heirs. The liquidator is responsible for inventorying the estate, paying debts, and distributing assets to heirs.

For real estate, the liquidator cannot simply transfer the property. The transfer must go through the notary, with all heirs consenting or a court order if consensus cannot be reached.

## The Three Options for Inherited Property

**Sell the property.** The most common outcome when multiple heirs are involved or when none of the heirs wants to manage a property. The sale proceeds are divided according to the succession agreement.

**Keep the property.** One or more heirs choose to retain the property, buying out co-heirs' shares at an agreed or appraised value. This requires the keeping heir(s) to have sufficient capital or financing capacity.

**Continue co-ownership.** Heirs decide to hold the property together for a period, typically to rent it for income or to wait for market conditions to improve before selling. This requires clear written agreements among co-heirs about management, cost sharing, and the conditions under which a future sale will be triggered.

## The Tax Considerations

Quebec and Canada treat inherited property differently from a tax perspective than some other jurisdictions. Key points:

**In Canada, there is no inheritance tax.** You do not pay tax simply because you received property through a will.

**There is a deemed disposition at death.** When a person dies, they are treated as having sold all their assets at fair market value on the date of death. Any capital gain on property (the difference between the property's value when the deceased acquired it and its value at death) is reported on the deceased's final tax return. This is the estate's tax obligation, not the heir's.

**The heir's cost basis is stepped up to the fair market value at death.** If you later sell the inherited property, your capital gain is calculated from the value at the date of inheritance, not from what the original owner paid. This can significantly reduce capital gains exposure on inherited properties that appreciated significantly during the deceased's ownership.

**The principal residence exemption.** If the property was the deceased's principal residence, a portion or all of the gain at death may be sheltered by the principal residence exemption.

Tax treatment is complex and varies by individual circumstances. Work with a tax professional before making decisions about inherited property.

## What to Do First When You Inherit Real Estate

**Do not rush.** Estate administration takes time. The pressure to sell quickly is often a mistake, particularly when emotional factors are influencing decisions.

**Get an independent market appraisal.** Regardless of whether you plan to sell or keep, understanding the property's value at the time of inheritance is essential for tax reporting, buyout negotiations among heirs, and informed decision-making.

**Understand the estate's obligations.** Are there outstanding mortgages? Property tax arrears? Unpaid maintenance? The property's net value is what matters, not just the gross market value.

**Consult a notary and a tax professional before making any transfers or decisions.** The legal and tax dimensions of inherited real estate in Quebec are specific enough that professional guidance is not optional.
