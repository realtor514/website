When I tell someone I'm a courtier immobilier, the response I most often get is "oh, a realtor?" The terms get used interchangeably in everyday conversation, but they mean different things - and in Quebec, the regulatory framework is specific enough that it's worth understanding.

## The Quebec Framework

In Quebec, real estate professionals are regulated by the **OACIQ** (Organisme d'autoréglementation du courtage immobilier du Québec). This body sets the licensing requirements, enforces professional standards, and handles complaints against real estate professionals.

The key designations under OACIQ:

**Courtier immobilier (real estate broker)**

This is the primary professional designation in Quebec. A courtier has completed the required education (AEC in real estate from a recognized institution), passed licensing examinations, and is registered with the OACIQ. A courtier can practice independently or work within a real estate agency (like RE/MAX DU CARTIER).

**Agence immobilière (real estate agency)**

A company that employs or contracts courtiers. RE/MAX DU CARTIER is an agence. The agency holds its own license and is responsible for supervising the brokers affiliated with it.

**Dirigeant d'agence (agency director)**

The certified professional responsible for the agency's operations and compliance. They hold an elevated certification level and are accountable to OACIQ for the agency's conduct.

## What About "Realtor"?

"Realtor" is a trademarked term in North America belonging to the National Association of Realtors (NAR) in the United States and the Canadian Real Estate Association (CREA) in Canada. Members of CREA who have earned the designation can use the term "Realtor."

In Quebec, many courtiers are also Realtors through CREA membership, but not all. The OACIQ certification is the legal requirement in Quebec; CREA membership is voluntary.

So "Realtor" is a subset of real estate professionals, not a synonym for all of them - and in Quebec, the OACIQ designation is more directly relevant to the professional standards your broker must meet.

## Why "Agent" Is Technically Incorrect in Quebec

In most Canadian provinces, the term "real estate agent" is used for licensed salespeople working under a broker. In Quebec, the proper term is "courtier" regardless of level. There is no formal "agent" designation in Quebec real estate regulation.

When you hear "agent" used in Quebec, it's usually informally, borrowed from usage in other provinces or from English-language media. The person in question is licensed as a courtier.

## What This Means Practically for Buyers and Sellers

**Your broker must be licensed with OACIQ.** This is non-negotiable. You can verify any Quebec real estate professional's license status at oaciq.com.

**Your broker owes you specific legal duties.** OACIQ requires that brokers act in your interest, provide complete and accurate information, avoid conflicts of interest, and maintain confidentiality. These are legal obligations, not voluntary commitments.

**Complaints have a formal channel.** If a broker fails to meet their obligations, OACIQ handles complaints and has the authority to discipline or revoke licenses. This protection exists because real estate is regulated.

**RE/MAX DU CARTIER and OACIQ oversight.** My affiliation with RE/MAX DU CARTIER means I operate under both RE/MAX standards and OACIQ regulation. That's two layers of professional accountability - which, in a field involving transactions of several hundred thousand dollars, is relevant.

## The Question to Ask Before Hiring Anyone

Before working with any real estate professional in Quebec, the single most important question: "Are you licensed with OACIQ and is your license currently active?"

You can verify the answer yourself at oaciq.com/en/find-a-broker. Takes 30 seconds.
