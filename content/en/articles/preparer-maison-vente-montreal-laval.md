The sellers who prepare their properties well consistently sell faster and for more money than those who don't. This is not a theory; it is visible in the data every single month.

But preparation doesn't mean spending $50,000 on renovations. It means being strategic about what buyers actually respond to. Here is what I tell every seller I work with before we list.

## Price Is the Most Important Decision You Make

Before any preparation, we need to talk about price, because it affects everything else.

A property priced correctly attracts buyers in the first 7 to 14 days on market. That initial window is when interest is highest. Buyers receive automatic alerts for new listings matching their criteria; your property gets maximum visibility precisely when you first appear on Centris.

If you price too high, you miss that window. After 30 days without an offer, buyers start wondering why nobody wants it. After 60 days, you've lost significant perceived value and often end up accepting a lower price than you would have received from the start.

The right price is not the highest price you can imagine. It is the highest price the market will actually pay today, for your specific property, based on what comparable homes have sold for in the past 90 days.

I analyze this data for every property I list. I don't tell sellers what they want to hear. I tell them what the market says.

## What to Fix Before Listing

You don't need to renovate. You need to remove every reason a buyer might hesitate.

**Fix what is broken.** Any active water infiltration must be resolved before listing; it is the single biggest red flag for any inspector and any buyer. Broken outlets, missing handles, doors that don't close properly, missing shingles visible from the street: these signal poor maintenance and invite buyers to negotiate harder.

**Deep clean everything.** This costs almost nothing and has one of the highest returns of any pre-sale investment. Clean the basement, the attic, the garage, the windows. Clean everything your cleaner normally skips.

**Neutralize the space.** The purple wall you love, the bold wallpaper in the master bedroom: these make it harder for buyers to picture themselves living there. Fresh paint in neutral tones (warm whites, light grays, soft beiges) costs $2,000 to $4,000 and generates one of the best returns on investment of any pre-sale improvement.

**Declutter ruthlessly.** Remove at least 30% of what's in every room. Buyers need to see the space, not your belongings.

## The Home Staging Reality

Home staging is not about decorating to your own taste; it is about presenting a space so that the maximum number of buyers can project themselves into it.

Statistically, well-staged homes sell 50% faster than un-staged equivalents, and they often sell closer to (or above) asking price. The investment is typically $1,500 to $3,000 for a professional staging consultation and rental of key pieces.

The return is almost always positive.

## Professional Photography Is Not Optional

More than 90% of buyers start their property search online. Your first showing is your photos.

Dark, blurry, or poorly composed photos send buyers scrolling to the next listing in two seconds. A professional real estate photographer costs $200 to $500. For a $600,000 property, that is less than 0.1% of the selling price.

Insist on it. If your agent wants to use their phone, that is the wrong agent.

For larger properties, drone photography adds significant value by showcasing the land, the surroundings, and the scale of the home.

## What NOT to Renovate Before Selling

Here is what surprises most sellers: the renovations with the lowest return are usually the most expensive ones.

A complete kitchen renovation at $40,000 to $60,000 rarely adds that same value to the sale price, because buyers will redo it to their own taste anyway. A luxurious bathroom renovation has the same problem.

The market pays for location, condition, and size. It pays a modest premium for cosmetic freshness. It does not pay full retail for expensive renovations completed to the seller's preferences.

Repair what is broken. Freshen what looks dated. Skip the full renovation.

## The Seller's Declaration

The seller's declaration (déclaration du vendeur) is one of the most important documents in the transaction, and one of the most poorly completed.

The temptation is to minimize problems so buyers aren't scared away. This is a serious mistake. A false or incomplete declaration can result in legal action years after the sale if hidden problems are discovered.

If there was a past water infiltration (even one that was properly repaired), declare it with the supporting documentation. Transparency with documentation actually builds buyer confidence. It says: there was an issue, here is how it was resolved, here is the proof.

Sellers who disclose proactively tend to sell faster and with fewer complications than those who hide and hope.

## Timing the Market

Spring (March to May) generates the highest volume of buyers and the most competitive conditions for sellers. But it also brings more properties to market, meaning more competition for your listing.

Fall (September to October) brings serious, motivated buyers with less competition from other sellers.

Winter is underrated: fewer properties compete for buyers, and the buyers who are active in December or January are highly motivated.

The best time to list is when your property is ready and the market in your specific area is active. A well-prepared property sells well in any season.

## The Bottom Line

The sellers who succeed are those who:

1. Price based on data, not hope
2. Fix visible defects and clean thoroughly
3. Invest in professional photography and basic staging
4. Complete their seller's declaration honestly and completely
5. Work with a broker who tells them the truth about the market

The preparation is straightforward. The discipline to follow through is rarer than you'd think.
