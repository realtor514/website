When buyers tell me they want "something more affordable than Montreal but still close enough to the city," the North Shore is almost always the answer. But the North Shore is not one market. It is a collection of distinct municipalities, each with a different character, infrastructure profile, and investment trajectory.

Here is my honest breakdown.

## Laval

Laval is the most urban of the North Shore municipalities and the one with the strongest infrastructure connection to Montreal. Served by three metro stations (Montmorency, Cartier, and De la Concorde), multiple highway access points, and increasingly by REM connectivity, Laval genuinely competes with many Montreal Island neighborhoods on commute time.

The residential market is diverse: dense urban sectors near the metro, established family neighborhoods in Duvernay and Laval-des-Rapides, village character in Sainte-Rose, and newer suburban developments in Auteuil and Saint-Francois.

Price point: typically 10-20% below comparable Montreal Island properties, with stronger appreciation trajectory than most buyers expect.

Best for: commuters, families, investors seeking rental yield, buyers who want more space per dollar.

## Terrebonne and Mascouche

These two municipalities sit east of Laval along the Riviere des Mille Iles and Autoroute 40. They offer significantly more space per dollar than Laval, newer housing stock (much of the development is recent), and a genuinely suburban character.

The trade-off is transit. Commuting from Terrebonne to downtown Montreal without a car requires the Exo commuter rail (Mascouche line), which runs limited service compared to the metro. If car independence matters to you, this is a significant consideration.

Best for: families who want newer construction, larger lots, and are comfortable with car-dependent lifestyles.

## Blainville and Sainte-Therese

Sainte-Therese is one of the most underrated markets in Greater Montreal. It has genuine village character with an active commercial center, good schools, and commuter rail access (Exo Saint-Jerome line) that makes Montreal commutes manageable. Blainville is newer, more suburban, and oriented around the Autoroute 15 corridor.

Both markets offer excellent value relative to Laval and significantly newer housing stock than many Montreal Island neighborhoods at comparable price points.

Best for: families prioritizing newer housing, buyers who commute by train, investors targeting family rental demand.

## Saint-Jerome and the Upper Laurentians

Saint-Jerome marks the entry point to the Laurentians and offers real estate prices that are genuinely surprising to buyers used to Montreal area pricing. The housing stock is mixed, the commercial center is evolving, and the Exo connection to Montreal exists but involves a longer commute.

This market attracts buyers who prioritize space and cost above all else, those who work remotely or have flexible commute schedules, and investors targeting vacation rental demand in the Laurentians corridor.

Best for: remote workers, buyers with very flexible commute requirements, investors in the vacation rental space.

## What I Look for in North Shore Properties

When I evaluate a North Shore property, the variables I weight most heavily are:

**Transit access:** Walking distance to metro, commuter rail, or major bus routes has a disproportionate impact on both rental demand and resale value.

**Highway access:** For car-dependent municipalities, proximity to interchange points matters significantly.

**School quality:** Family-oriented markets live and die by their school catchment reputation.

**Development trajectory:** Is new commercial and residential development moving toward this sector or away from it?

**Infrastructure investment:** Municipalities that are actively investing in parks, community centers, and commercial corridors are positioning for appreciation.

The North Shore is not a consolation prize for buyers who cannot afford Montreal. For the right buyer with clear priorities, it is often the superior choice on every dimension that actually matters.
