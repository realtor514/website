"Save 20% before you buy. Never pay mortgage insurance."

This advice gets passed down like family wisdom. It sounds prudent. It is sometimes right. But it is not a universal truth, and for many buyers in the Greater Montreal market, following it rigidly means leaving significant wealth on the table.

Let me show you the math.

## What CMHC Mortgage Insurance Actually Costs

When your down payment is less than 20%, Canada requires you to purchase default mortgage insurance through CMHC or a private insurer. The premium is:

- **5-9.99% down:** 4% of the mortgage amount
- **10-14.99% down:** 3.1% of the mortgage amount
- **15-19.99% down:** 2.8% of the mortgage amount

On a $500,000 home with 10% down ($50,000), the insurance premium is: $450,000 × 3.1% = **$13,950** - added to your mortgage, not paid upfront.

The monthly impact of this premium over 25 years is approximately $75-80/month extra in mortgage payments.

## The Question Nobody Asks: What Else Would That Capital Do?

Here's where the analysis gets interesting. Suppose you have $50,000 saved. You could:

**Option A:** Wait 2-3 more years to save another $50,000 to reach 20% down on a $500,000 property.

**Option B:** Buy now with 10% down, pay the insurance premium, and let the property build equity while you continue saving.

During those 2-3 waiting years:
- The Montreal market has historically appreciated at roughly 4-6% annually over the long term
- On a $500,000 property, that's $20,000-$30,000 per year in appreciation you don't capture
- Meanwhile, you're continuing to pay rent with no equity accumulation

If the property appreciates $50,000 while you're waiting to save that same $50,000, you've run in place. The 20% target keeps moving away from you as prices rise.

## When 20% Down Actually Makes Sense

I'm not saying the 20% rule is always wrong. Here are the cases where it is the right strategy:

**When the market is flat or declining.** If prices aren't rising, the opportunity cost of waiting decreases. You're not losing appreciation by waiting.

**When your income is variable or uncertain.** A larger down payment means a smaller mortgage and more financial cushion if your income drops. That matters.

**When you're buying at or near your financial ceiling.** If 5% down means your monthly payment consumes 45% of your income, the financial fragility risk is real.

**When the insurance premium changes your qualification.** Sometimes the insured premium pushes your total borrowing cost above what makes sense for your return expectations.

## The Real Calculation

Instead of asking "should I put 20% down?", ask:

1. What is my monthly payment difference between 10% and 20% down?
2. How long would it take me to save the extra 10%?
3. What would the property's value likely be in that same period?
4. What would my total return look like under each scenario?

This is an engineering approach to a financial decision, and it leads to better outcomes than inherited rules of thumb.

## My Honest Advice

For most first-time buyers in Greater Montreal who have stable income, a solid emergency fund, and realistic expectations about the property they're buying: 5-10% down and buy now is often the superior financial strategy compared to waiting.

For buyers who are stretching to their limit, carrying significant existing debt, or buying in a market with weak fundamentals: wait, save more, strengthen your position.

The answer depends on your situation. Anyone who tells you the answer is the same for everyone isn't doing the analysis.
