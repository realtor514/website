Property taxes are not complicated, but they are frequently misunderstood and consistently underestimated in buyers' budgets. Here is a complete explanation of how they work in Quebec.

## The Municipal Evaluation vs. Market Value

Every property in Quebec has a municipal evaluation (valeur municipale) established by the municipality through a triennial evaluation process. This evaluation is conducted by independent appraisers contracted by the municipality and typically reflects market values as of approximately 18 months before the evaluation roll takes effect.

The municipal evaluation is not the same as market value. In active appreciating markets, municipal evaluations often lag behind true market value. The gap can be significant: a property with a $450,000 municipal evaluation may trade at $600,000 in a strong market.

Your property taxes are calculated based on the municipal evaluation, not on what you paid.

## How the Tax Rate Works

Municipalities publish a property tax rate expressed in dollars per $100 of municipal evaluation. To calculate your annual taxes:

(Municipal evaluation / 100) x tax rate = annual property tax

Example: Municipal evaluation of $500,000, tax rate of $1.00 per $100:
($500,000 / 100) x $1.00 = $5,000 annually

Tax rates vary by municipality and by property type. In Montreal, the island municipality applies different rates to residential, commercial, and multi-unit properties. In Laval, a unified city applies its own rate schedule.

## School Tax

In Quebec, a separate school tax (taxe scolaire) is levied annually by the school service center in your area. This tax is calculated separately from municipal property taxes but is also based on the municipal evaluation. The school tax rate is significantly lower than the municipal rate; the annual bill is typically a few hundred dollars for most residential properties.

## The Welcome Tax (One-Time at Purchase)

When you purchase a property in Quebec, you pay a one-time municipal transfer tax called the taxe de bienvenue (welcome tax). This is not an annual tax, but it is substantial enough to plan for.

The welcome tax is calculated on a sliding scale:
- 0.5% on the first $58,900 of the property value
- 1.0% on the portion between $58,900 and $294,600
- 1.5% on the portion above $294,600

For a $550,000 purchase, the welcome tax is approximately $7,200-$7,500.

Some municipalities apply additional surtaxes on higher-value properties. The City of Montreal applies additional rates above $500,000 that increase the welcome tax for higher-end purchases.

## How Municipal Evaluations Change

Every three years, municipalities issue a new evaluation roll. When evaluations increase significantly (as happened in many Quebec municipalities following the appreciation cycle of recent years), property tax bills can increase sharply even if the tax rate stays constant.

Homeowners can contest their municipal evaluation if they believe it is inaccurate. The contestation process involves filing with the Bureau de revision de l'evaluation fonciere. Most successful contests involve providing recent comparable sales data showing that the evaluation exceeds actual market value.

## What This Means for Buyers

When evaluating a property, calculate the property taxes from the municipal evaluation that will apply after your purchase, not from what the current owner is paying. If the property was purchased years ago and the evaluation was not updated to reflect a subsequent sale, the current owner may be paying taxes based on a lower evaluation than you will face.

Budget conservatively: $4,000-$8,000 annually for property taxes on a $500,000-$650,000 residential property in Laval or Montreal, depending on municipality and exact property. School tax adds $300-$600 typically.
